**Q2 — UK GDPR enforcement reality**

- The ICO publicly shifted toward reprimands rather than monetary penalties for public-sector and small-organisation cases starting around 2022; the policy has been described in ICO blogs as proportionate enforcement. **Confidence: medium — exact policy text needs verification with current ICO guidance.**
- Most large GDPR-Article-13 / lawful-basis enforcement against small UK firms in recent years has been against marketing/PECR violations (cold-calling, unsolicited email) rather than privacy-notice-only failures. **Confidence: medium-low — needs cross-check with ICO enforcement registry.**
- The £17.5M / 4% turnover ceiling in the idea doc is the statutory maximum under UK GDPR Article 83 — **high confidence on the maximum, very low on the realistic exposure for a sub-seed B2C app.**
- ICO publishes an enforcement action register at ico.org.uk that allows filtering by type (MPN, reprimand, enforcement notice). The deepening pass should query this directly. **Confidence: high (the source exists).**
- The brief's framing "is the proposed hard-block too conservative?" has an asymmetric-cost answer worth flagging: even if enforcement risk is low, a wrong privacy notice is also a contractual/B2B-buyer risk (DPAs require accurate notices) and a Companies-House-disclosure-style reputational risk. The deepening pass should distinguish ICO enforcement risk from contract/buyer risk. **Confidence: medium-high.**

**Q3 — SEIS/EIS qualification**

- SEIS limits (as of last well-confirmed point, 2023 reform): £250k lifetime company raise, £200k investor annual subscription, gross assets ≤£350k pre-money, ≤25 full-time-equivalent employees, company ≤3 years old; EIS limits: £12M lifetime general / £20M knowledge-intensive, ≤250 employees / ≤500 KIC, gross assets ≤£15M pre / ≤£16M post. **Needs verification against current 2026 thresholds.**
- HMRC VCM manuals — VCM31000 series covers EIS (income-tax relief), VCM34000 series covers SEIS-specific rules; VCM31100 onwards covers qualifying companies/trades. **Confidence: high on series naming, medium on exact section numbers.**
- Top retroactive-disqualification triggers (high confidence on category, medium on completeness):
  1. Wrong share class — SEIS/EIS shares must be ordinary, fully paid, no preferential rights to dividend or assets on winding-up. Issuing shares with liquidation preferences voids relief.
  2. Value extraction — repaying loans to investors, providing benefits, certain related-party transactions during the 3-year period.
  3. Qualifying-trade drift — the company must carry on a qualifying trade for 3 years; "non-qualifying activities" exceeding 20% void it (financial activities, dealing in land/commodities, leasing, legal/accountancy, hotels, nursing homes).
  4. Control changes — losing independence (becoming a subsidiary), disqualifying acquisition.
  5. Sub-£250k SEIS or pre-money valuation issues; or shares issued before HMRC compliance certificate (SEIS1/EIS1) submission.
- **Advance Assurance** — non-statutory; HMRC reviews company plan and confirms in writing that the company *appears* to qualify. Average turnaround historically ~4–8 weeks. **Confidence: medium on turnaround; needs current verification.**

**Q4 — UK employment law / IR35**

- ERA 1996 s86 statutory minimum notice: 1 week for ≥1 month service, then 1 week per year of service to a max of 12 weeks (employer to employee). **Confidence: high (statutory, stable).**
- Working Time Regulations 1998: 5.6 weeks (28 days for full-time) statutory paid leave including bank holidays. **Confidence: high.**
- SSP rate £116.75/week (2024-25); 2025-26 / 2026-27 rates need verification — DWP uprates annually in April. **Confidence: medium.**
- NI/PAYE registration with HMRC required before first paycheque; right-to-work checks under Immigration, Asylum and Nationality Act 2006 s15 are mandatory and now permit IDVT for British/Irish passport holders. **Confidence: high on requirement, medium on current IDVT scope.**
- Pension auto-enrolment (Pensions Act 2008) applies once a UK employer pays anyone earning ≥£10,000/year aged 22–SPA. Trigger thresholds change annually. **Confidence: high on framework, medium on current threshold.**
- IR35 / off-payroll-working: small-company exemption under ITEPA 2003 Ch 10 — if the client meets the Companies Act 2006 small-company test (any 2 of: turnover ≤£10.2M, balance sheet ≤£5.1M, ≤50 employees), the determination obligation stays with the worker's PSC, not the client. Most seed-stage startups qualify as small. **Confidence: high on existence of exemption, medium on whether thresholds have been uprated post-2024 (Companies Act small-company thresholds were increased ~2024 — needs verification).**
- HMRC CEST tool factors: mutuality of obligation, personal service / right of substitution, control, financial risk, integration. CEST has been criticised by the Lords' Economic Affairs Committee and lost in tribunal cases (HMRC v Atholl House) — using CEST output alone is risky. **Confidence: medium.**
- Hard-block-to-lawyer surface: anyone working substantially full-time, exclusive, under direction, with no substitution right, integrated into the team, on a regular wage — should NOT be templated as a contractor; that's the named "silent-wrongness" failure mode in the idea doc. **Confidence: high on principle.**

**Q5 — Jurisdiction expansion marginal cost**

- US "work made for hire" doctrine (17 USC §101) covers only nine specific commissioned-work categories plus employment; **commissioned software is generally NOT a WMFH-eligible category** unless the contractor is an employee. Best US practice mirrors UK best practice: explicit present-tense assignment-of-rights clause. So the IP-assignment template prose is largely portable; what changes is governing-law/venue and the WMFH-recital paragraph. **Confidence: high.**
- US T&Cs differ from UK T&Cs primarily in consumer-rights regime (Magnuson-Moss vs Consumer Rights Act 2015), arbitration-clause norms, choice-of-law/venue, and DMCA-vs-EU-NTD safe harbour for hosted content. Pure prose reuse: ~40–60%; structural reuse: high. **Confidence: medium.**
- EU multi-state employment law: meaningful per-state divergence on notice, probation, dismissal protection, working time. Templating "EU" as a single jurisdiction is a category error. The natural seam is `eu-<de|fr|es|nl|...>.md`. GDPR is the only cleanly-shared layer. **Confidence: high.**
- Stripe Atlas added UK incorporation as a separate product; SeedLegals expanded to France and Hong Kong with separate jurisdiction codebases (their public engineering blog has discussed this). Clerky chose to remain US-only. The pattern across the three is: **second jurisdiction costs more like 60–80% of the first, not 20%, because the legal substrate is independent.** **Confidence: medium — needs verification with public statements.**
- Verdict orientation (subject to Q5c verification): **"UK now, deal with US/EU as v2 architecture pivot"** is the lower-risk default; the matrix-from-day-one design risks premature abstraction without empirical demand. **Confidence: medium.**
